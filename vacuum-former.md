## What this machine is used for

A vacuum former shapes heated plastic sheets over a mold using vacuum pressure.

The plastic sheet is clamped, heated until soft, pulled over a mold, and vacuumed tightly around the mold shape. After cooling, the formed plastic keeps the shape of the mold.

Common uses:

- Packaging prototypes
- Product covers
- Plastic shells
- Display trays
- Molds
- Costume and cosplay parts
- Educational models
- Design prototypes
- Protective covers
- Transparent covers
- Small batch plastic parts
- Formed plastic art and design pieces

Vacuum forming is useful when a project needs a thin plastic shape instead of a solid 3D printed object.

## Basic Rules

- Use the vacuum former only after instruction or staff permission.
- Use only approved plastic sheets.
- Use only suitable molds.
- Do not touch the heater area.
- Do not touch hot plastic with bare hands.
- Keep hands away from the clamp frame and moving parts.
- Check that the mold is stable before forming.
- Do not use wet, dirty, or unknown plastic.
- Do not use molds that can melt, collapse, or release toxic fumes.
- Stay near the machine during heating and forming.
- Clean the forming area after use.
- Report damaged seals, weak vacuum, unusual smell, overheating, or machine errors.

## Safety Notes

Vacuum forming includes heat, pressure, moving parts, hot plastic, and possible fumes.

Main risks:

- Hot heater
- Hot plastic sheet
- Burns
- Pinch points from clamp frame
- Sharp plastic edges after trimming
- Plastic fumes
- Mold failure
- Weak vacuum causing failed forming
- Electrical risk if machine is damaged
- Compressed air risk
- Tripping or clutter around the machine

Safe behavior:

- Keep hands away from hot and moving parts.
- Wear heat-resistant gloves when handling hot materials if needed.
- Wear safety glasses.
- Use only approved plastics.
- Check ventilation if plastic smell is strong.
- Do not leave heating plastic unattended.
- Allow formed plastic to cool before handling.
- Trim formed plastic carefully because edges can be sharp.
- Keep the work area clean and dry.

## Mold Materials

The mold must tolerate heat and vacuum pressure.

Possible mold materials:

- MDF
- Plywood
- Solid wood
- 3D printed plastic, if heat-resistant enough
- Resin printed molds, if approved
- Machined plastic
- Aluminum
- Plaster, if sealed and dry
- Foam, only if approved and heat-safe

Mold material must be:

- Dry
- Clean
- Stable
- Strong enough
- Heat-resistant enough
- Free from loose dust
- Smooth enough for release
- Designed with draft angle

---

## Mold Design Rules

Good mold design is important for successful vacuum forming.

Recommended mold design:

- Add draft angle to vertical walls.
- Avoid perfectly vertical sides.
- Round sharp corners.
- Avoid deep narrow cavities.
- Avoid undercuts unless the part can be removed.
- Add small air holes in deep areas if needed.
- Make the mold surface smooth.
- Seal porous materials such as MDF or plaster.
- Make the mold strong enough for vacuum pressure.
- Keep mold height within machine capacity.
- Leave enough space around the mold for plastic stretching.

Good draft angle helps the formed plastic release from the mold.

## File Preparation

Vacuum forming does not usually use a direct digital file like 3D printing or laser cutting.

Instead, the digital file is usually used to make the mold.

Possible mold-making methods:

- 3D printing
- CNC routing
- CNC milling
- Laser cutting stacked layers
- Hand shaping
- Casting
- Machining

Common digital workflow:

1. Design the target shape in CAD.
2. Design the mold from the target shape.
3. Add draft angle and rounded corners.
4. Check mold height and forming depth.
5. Make the mold using CNC, 3D printing, or hand tools.
6. Smooth and seal the mold if needed.
7. Test with a small or simple sheet first.
8. Record forming result.

Common CAD software:

- Fusion 360
- SolidWorks
- Blender
- Rhino
- FreeCAD
- Onshape
- Tinkercad

## Vacuum Forming Workflow

### 1. Prepare the mold

Before forming:

- Check mold size.
- Check mold height.
- Check draft angle.
- Check surface smoothness.
- Check for undercuts.
- Check if air holes are needed.
- Make sure the mold is dry and clean.
- Place the mold correctly on the forming table.

### 2. Prepare the plastic sheet

Check:

- Material type
- Sheet thickness
- Sheet size
- Surface cleanliness
- Protective film
- Scratches or cracks

Remove protective film if required before heating.

### 3. Clamp the sheet

- Place the plastic sheet in the clamp frame.
- Make sure it is centered.
- Make sure the clamp is secure.
- Check that the sheet cannot slip during heating.

### 4. Heat the sheet

- Move the heater over the sheet.
- Heat until the sheet softens and sags correctly.
- Watch the sheet during heating.
- Do not overheat the plastic.

Heating time depends on:

- Plastic type
- Sheet thickness
- Heater settings
- Room temperature
- Desired forming detail

### 5. Form the part

- Move the softened sheet over the mold.
- Apply vacuum.
- Let the plastic pull tightly over the mold.
- Use pre-stretch if needed for deeper molds.
- Use auto-level according to machine setup and staff instruction.

### 6. Cool the part

- Keep the part in place until it cools enough.
- Do not remove too early.
- Avoid warping during cooling.

### 7. Remove the formed part

- Release vacuum.
- Remove the plastic carefully.
- Avoid cracking the formed plastic.
- Remove the mold carefully.

### 8. Trim and finish

After forming:

- Trim excess plastic.
- Sand sharp edges if needed.
- Drill holes if needed.
- Check fit and surface quality.
- Record result.

## Common Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Possible Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Poor detail | Sheet not hot enough, weak vacuum, bad mold venting | Heat longer, check vacuum, add vent holes if needed |
| Webbing | Mold too tall, molds too close, poor layout | Increase spacing, reduce mold height, use pre-stretch |
| Plastic tears | Overheated sheet, sharp mold corners, too much stretching | Reduce heat time, round mold corners, use thicker sheet |
| Plastic too thin at corners | Deep draw, poor mold design, no pre-stretch | Use pre-stretch, reduce mold height, improve mold shape |
| Bubbles in sheet | Moisture in plastic, overheating | Use dry material, reduce heat, check storage |
| Sheet does not sag evenly | Uneven heating, bad clamping, wrong heater setting | Check clamp, adjust heating zones, heat evenly |
| Sheet slips from clamp | Clamp not tight, wrong sheet size | Re-clamp correctly, use correct sheet size |
| Mold sticks inside part | No draft angle, undercut, rough surface | Add draft angle, remove undercuts, smooth mold |
| Weak vacuum | Leak, bad seal, blocked holes, pump issue | Check seal, table, mold holes, and vacuum system |
| Part warps after removal | Removed too hot, uneven cooling, thin material | Let cool longer, support part during cooling |
| Surface has marks | Dirty sheet, dirty mold, rough mold | Clean material and mold, smooth mold surface |
| Bad smell or fumes | Wrong material, overheating, poor ventilation | Stop process, check material, ventilate area |

## Mold Release Notes

If the formed part is difficult to remove:

- Check for undercuts.
- Add draft angle.
- Smooth the mold.
- Use a release method approved by GerLab staff.
- Avoid rough porous surfaces.
- Avoid sharp mold edges.
- Let the plastic cool before removal.
- Do not force removal if the part may crack.

---

## Plastic Trimming Notes

After forming, the plastic part usually needs trimming.

Possible trimming tools:

- Utility knife
- Scissors
- Rotary tool
- Bandsaw, if approved
- Laser cutter, only if material is laser-safe and staff approve it
- CNC router, if suitable
- Sandpaper or deburring tool

Safety:

- Wear safety glasses.
- Cut away from your hand.
- Watch sharp plastic edges.
- Clamp the part if needed.
- Do not laser cut unknown plastic.

3. Check mold draft angle.
4. Check plastic material and thickness.
5. Place mold on forming table.
6. Clamp plastic sheet.
7. Heat sheet until it softens evenly.
8. Apply vacuum and form over mold.
9. Let the part cool.
10. Release and remove formed plastic.
11. Trim excess plastic.
12. Inspect final result.
13. Clean workspace.
14. Record useful settings and result.

## Good Practice

Before forming:

- Start with a simple mold.
- Use rounded corners.
- Avoid undercuts.
- Use draft angle.
- Keep mold height reasonable.
- Use clean, dry plastic.
- Test with cheaper material first.
- Watch the plastic carefully while heating.
- Stop if smell or smoke is unusual.
- Record successful forming conditions.
