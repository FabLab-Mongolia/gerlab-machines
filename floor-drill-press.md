## What this machine is used for

The floor drill press rotates a cutting tool while the workpiece remains fixed to the table.

Common uses:

- Drilling accurate holes
- Spot drilling
- Countersinking
- Counterboring with approved tools
- Reaming
- Hole-saw work within approved capacity
- Tapping only with staff-approved tooling and procedure
- Preparing metal, plastic, or wood components for fabrication

---

## Basic Rules

- Use the drill press only after training or staff permission.
- Clamp every workpiece with a vise, clamp, or approved fixture.
- Never hold material by hand.
- Remove the chuck key immediately after tightening.
- Select the correct tool and spindle speed.
- Keep the belt cover and guards closed.
- Keep hair, sleeves, jewelry, gloves, and lanyards away from the spindle.
- Stop the spindle before measuring, adjusting, or clearing chips.
- Do not use excessive force.
- Stop if the tool slips, bends, chatters, or grabs the workpiece.

---

## Safety Notes

Main risks:

- Entanglement with the spindle, chuck, or tool
- Workpiece spinning or being thrown
- Tool breakage
- Flying chips
- Pinch points
- Sharp swarf and burrs
- Unexpected quill movement
- Chuck key ejection

Safe behavior:

- Tie back long hair and remove loose items.
- Wear safety glasses and closed shoes.
- Do not wear gloves during drilling.
- Clamp the workpiece to resist both rotation and lifting.
- Use a backing board or sacrificial plate where appropriate.
- Reduce feed as the tool breaks through.
- Use a brush or chip hook after the spindle stops.
- Isolate power before belt changes or maintenance.

---

## Personal Protective Equipment

- Safety glasses
- Closed shoes
- Hearing protection when required
- Face shield when the task assessment requires it

Do not wear gloves near the rotating spindle, chuck, or tool.

## Prohibited or Restricted Materials

- Unknown material
- Material that cannot be clamped securely
- Round stock without a suitable vise or fixture
- Thin sheet without proper support and clamping
- Cracked or unstable workpieces
- Pressurized or sealed containers
- Hardened material without approved tooling
- Carbon fiber, fiberglass, or hazardous composites without approved controls
- Oversized or excessively heavy workpieces

## Cutting Tools

Possible tools include:

- Twist drill
- Center drill or spot drill
- Step drill
- Countersink
- Reamer
- Hole saw with approved setup
- Tap with approved tapping method

Check that the tool is sharp, straight, correctly seated, and suitable for the chuck or taper.

## Workholding

Approved workholding may include:

- Drill-press vise
- T-slot clamps
- V-block for round stock
- Dedicated fixture
- Backing board or sacrificial plate

The workpiece must resist rotation, lifting, and lateral movement. Hand pressure is not workholding.

---

## Speed and Feed Notes

Spindle speed depends on:

- Material
- Tool material
- Tool diameter
- Operation type
- Coolant or lubricant method

Larger tools normally require lower speed. Harder materials normally require lower speed. Use the machine speed chart or a staff-approved calculation; do not guess.

---

## Drilling Workflow

1. Inspect the machine, guards, chuck, table, vise, and controls.
2. Identify material, hole size, and required depth.
3. Select the correct tool and spindle speed.
4. Isolate power before changing belt position where required.
5. Install the tool and remove the chuck key immediately.
6. Position and clamp the workpiece securely.
7. Set table height and depth stop where needed.
8. Apply approved cutting fluid if required.
9. Start the spindle and observe for tool runout.
10. Feed smoothly without forcing the tool.
11. Retract periodically for chip control when appropriate.
12. Reduce feed at breakthrough.
13. Stop the spindle completely.
14. Remove chips with a brush or tool.
15. Deburr and inspect the hole.
16. Clean the machine and table.

## Common Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Possible cause | Safe response |
|---|---|---|
| Tool chatters | Loose work, dull tool, wrong speed, excessive extension | Stop and correct setup |
| Hole is oversized | Tool runout, damaged drill, poor starting point | Inspect chuck, tool, and setup |
| Workpiece spins | Inadequate clamping | Stop immediately; never grab the workpiece |
| Tool overheats | Wrong speed, poor lubrication, excessive feed | Stop, allow cooling, and review settings |
| Drill grabs at breakthrough | Excessive feed or poor support | Reduce feed and improve support |
| Chuck slips | Tool not seated or chuck problem | Stop and have the chuck inspected |

---

## Maintenance Notes

- Clean chips from the table, vise, column, and floor.
- Keep the table and column lightly protected from corrosion.
- Inspect chuck, arbor, belts, guards, and power cable.
- Check table and head locks.
- Report excessive runout, vibration, unusual noise, or slipping belts.
- Lubrication, belt replacement, alignment, and electrical work are staff tasks.
